  \begin{block}{Background \& Motivation}

    Women in underserved urban communities face substantial barriers to
    preventive cancer screening, leading to late-stage diagnoses and avoidable
    mortality. At NewYork-Presbyterian (NYP), screening programs for cervical,
    lung, breast, colorectal, and bone-density cancers operate through
    \textbf{fragmented, siloed workflows} that create scheduling bottlenecks
    and high rates of loss-to-follow-up (LTFU).

    Key gaps driving this work:

    \begin{itemize}
      \item \textbf{Appointment delays} — Women with abnormal results wait
            weeks to months for follow-up colposcopy or biopsy.
      \item \textbf{Coordinated care deficits} — Patients navigating multiple
            specialties encounter redundant intake and hand-off failures.
      \item \textbf{Screening uptake gaps} — Lung LDCT utilization among
            eligible smokers remains below 15\% nationally despite USPSTF
            Grade B recommendation \cite{amicizia2025}.
      \item \textbf{Resource scarcity} — Colposcopy capacity is a documented
            bottleneck; longer waits are directly associated with LTFU
            \cite{bonafede2019}.
    \end{itemize}

  \end{block}

  \begin{block}{USPSTF Guidelines Modeled}

    Eligibility and test assignment follow U.S.\ Preventive Services Task
    Force (USPSTF) recommendations for five cancer/disease types:

    \begin{itemize}
      \item \textbf{Cervical} — Pap cytology every 3 yr (age 21--29);
            hrHPV-alone or co-test every 5 yr (age 30--65)
      \item \textbf{Lung} — Annual low-dose CT (LDCT) for smokers aged
            50--80 with $\geq$20 pack-year history
      \item \textbf{Breast} — Biennial mammography, age 40--74
      \item \textbf{Colorectal} — Colonoscopy or FIT, age 45--75
      \item \textbf{Osteoporosis} — DEXA, age 65+ or BMI $<$19
    \end{itemize}

  \end{block}

  \begin{alertblock}{Research Objectives}

    \begin{enumerate}
      \item Quantify current-state screening uptake, wait times, and LTFU
            across five cancer types at NYP.
      \item Identify scheduling and capacity bottlenecks most strongly
            associated with patient dropout.
      \item Model a \textbf{coordinated-care scenario} and estimate its
            impact on access and outcomes relative to the fragmented
            current state.
    \end{enumerate}

  \end{alertblock}

\end{column}

  \begin{block}{Simulation Architecture}

    The model is implemented in \textbf{Python / SimPy} as a discrete-event
    simulation (DES) with a stable cycling population of \textbf{15{,}000
    patients} over a \textbf{70-year horizon}. All parameters live in a
    central \texttt{config.py}; events are logged to a SQLite database for
    post-hoc analysis \cite{kuruppu2025}.

    \heading{Three-Queue Scheduling Model}

    \begin{itemize}
      \item \textbf{Outpatient queue} — Scheduled PCP/GYN appointments;
            inter-arrival from empirical NYP distributions.
      \item \textbf{Drop-in queue} — Walk-in visits; Poisson arrivals
            calibrated to clinic volumes.
      \item \textbf{Follow-up queue} — Priority-weighted return visits
            triggered by abnormal results; urgency class set by result
            severity.
    \end{itemize}

    SimPy \texttt{Resource} objects enforce capacity at each service point:
    colposcopy (8 slots/day), LEEP (5 slots/day), LDCT (12 slots/day),
    and mammography.

    \heading{Cervical Pathway (Fully Specified)}

    Result classification follows ASCCP guidelines. Downstream actions:

    \begin{center}
    \begin{tabular}{ll}
      \toprule
      \textbf{Result} & \textbf{Follow-up action} \\
      \midrule
      Normal            & Return to scheduled interval \\
      ASCUS / LSIL      & Reflex HPV; if +, colposcopy \\
      ASC-H / HSIL      & Expedited colposcopy \\
      HPV+ / normal cyto & Colposcopy or 1-yr repeat \\
      CIN 1             & 1-yr surveillance \\
      CIN 2 / CIN 3     & LEEP or cold-knife cone \\
      \bottomrule
    \end{tabular}
    \end{center}

  \end{block}

  \begin{block}{Scenario Analysis: Coordinated vs.\ Fragmented Care}

    Two workflow scenarios are compared via a single configuration flag
    (\texttt{WORKFLOW\_MODE = "fragmented"} vs.\ \texttt{"coordinated"}).
    The coordinated scenario increases colposcopy capacity by 50\% and
    reduces LTFU base rates, reflecting automated recall and care-coordinator
    support.

    \heading{Preliminary Scenario Comparison (15-Year Horizon)}

    \begin{itemize}
      \item \textbf{LTFU} post-abnormal screen: $18\% \to 10\%$
            \quad ($-8$ percentage points)
      \item \textbf{Colposcopy completion}: $63\% \to 77\%$
      \item \textbf{Median colposcopy wait}: $32 \to 19$ days ($-41\%$)
      \item \textbf{Cervical uptake} (30--65 band): $+5$ pp
    \end{itemize}

    These represent upper-bound estimates; real-world gains depend on
    phased capacity investment and staff training timelines.

  \end{block}

  \begin{block}{Future Work}

    \begin{itemize}
      \item \textbf{EHR calibration} — Integrate NYP administrative claims
            and electronic health record data to ground-truth all parameters.
      \item \textbf{Health equity stratification} — Disaggregate outcomes
            by race, ethnicity, insurance status, and borough to identify
            disparate impacts.
      \item \textbf{Full 70-year horizon} — Scale to full simulation
            duration for lifetime cancer incidence projections.
      \item \textbf{Multi-cancer pathways} — Extend breast, colorectal,
            and osteoporosis stubs to fully specified clinical pathways.
      \item \textbf{Cost-effectiveness} — Link outputs to cost-per-QALY
            estimates to support policy recommendation.
    \end{itemize}

  \end{block}
